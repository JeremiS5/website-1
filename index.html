Video games and the mind

IT U4 SAT by Jeremy van Nispen-Struhs

Scroll down

Summary

I chose to study the benefits that playing video games can have on the human brain. I chose this topic because I’m interested in it, and because I believe that playing video games can have substantial benefits. I also wanted to refute biases by providing information. I searched Google Scholar to find scientific studies related to my topic. I also watched several TED talks and read newspaper articles.

Hypothesis

My hypothesis was:
Playing video games regularly leads to improved mental functioning and academic performance because of a number of variables such as the act of working as a team, strategic thinking, and deductive reasoning, among others.

I studied:
  • The mental benefits of playing games
  • Academic performance increase from playing games
  • Further life advantages from learning strategic thinking, deductive reasoning and teamwork skills at a young age
  • Mental health, social skills, work skills, learning skills, cognitive functioning, improving memory.
  • Benefits in cognitive functioning from playing video games include: reaction times, calculation speeds, memory recollection and storing, puzzle solving, hand-eye coordination, decision making
  • Benefits of playing video games for young children include: learning about social constructs, literacy, spelling and grammar skills, logic skills development


Scope

My scope was:
I studied teenagers in Australia, specifically Melbourne, at Aquinas College. I studied whether or not gamers get better marks on average than non-gamers, and whether they think that playing games improves their marks or not.

Findings

My key findings were:
Gaming has many benefits to the mind and for learning, including but not limited to:

  • Sensory benefits such as improved visual contrast sensitivity, higher ability to detect track moving objects in a field of distractors, and improvements in hand eye coordination
  • Cognitive benefits such as improved reaction time, improved brain plasticity, improved social skills, and improvements in ability to multitask
  • And general health benefits such as treatment of depression, treatment of amblyopia (lazy eye), treatment of brain injuries and illnesses.


"In a series of experiments on small numbers of gamers (10 to 14 people in each study), the researchers reported that gamers with previous experience of playing such action video games were better at perceptual tasks such as pattern discrimination than gamers with less experience.
In another experiment, they trained gamers that had little previous experience of playing action games, giving them 50 hours practice. It was showed that these gamers performed much better on perceptual tasks than they had prior to their training."
~ Mark Griffiths, The Conversation (source)

Survey results:
  • The majority of year 10, 11, and 12 IT students play video games
  • The majority of those students play on PC
  • The genre of games they most often play is widely varied, but the most common is Role Playing game series such as The Elder Scrolls, Pokemon, Fallout and the Witcher.
  • The majority of them estimate that they play 11-20 hours of games per week.
  • There is an even split between the students that believe that playing games is beneficial to their brain, and those who don’t.
  • The majority of students play video games for fun, rather than to socialise or learn.
  • The majority of students believe that playing games causes them to receive worse marks at school.
  • All students who believed that playing games caused them to receive better marks at school also believe that playing games improves brain function.
  • The students that believed that playing games made their marks worse generally say that the time would be better spent studying, which they believe would result in better marks.
  • The majority of the students that believed that playing games made their marks worse believe that playing games improves brain function.
  • Almost all students believe that children can benefit by playing video games.
  • The majority of students believe that playing games can benefit treatment of mental illnesses and disorders.

Conclusion

Playing video games is clearly highly beneficial to those of all ages and situations, as long as the person playing has the self control not to play games so much that it interferes with their quality of life, meaning that it interrupts healthy sleep cycles or causes less work or education to be done, or causes them to withdraw socially.

My hypothesis is accurate and correct. There is substantial evidence from my research that playing games directly leads to improved mental functioning.
